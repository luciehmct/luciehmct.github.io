    The feedback loop of violence: How violent movies shape real-world violence

    EPFL Project

    Context: This project investigated whether the prevalence of violent movies correlates with real-world violence in the US. We analyzed 17,000+ movies (from CMU and Kaggle datasets) against US crime statistics (from the NIBRS dataset). To isolate the impact, we implemented an auto-regressive distributed lag (ARDL) model with time-fixed effects. Our study found no statistically significant correlation based on the available data.

    What I implemented

    • Managed the complete data pipeline for all real-world violence data, using the NIBRS dataset.
    • Performed extensive data exploration, extraction, and cleaning to prepare the raw, daily-level crime statistics for time-series analysis.
    • Engineered the primary "violence score," a weekly aggregated metric that served as the key dependent variable for the team's ARDL model.
    • Created plots and visualizations to analyze and present the trends and patterns in the real-world violence data over time.

    My contributions

    In this team project, my primary responsibility was owning the "real-world violence" half of the equation. I sourced, processed, and modeled all crime data from the NIBRS database, transforming raw daily reports into a clean, robust weekly time-series. The "violence score" I engineered was the core variable our team used to test our hypothesis. I also produced the visualizations for this data, which helped inform the ARDL model's design and interpret the final results.
